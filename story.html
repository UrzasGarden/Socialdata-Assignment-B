SF Crime Data Story

The Rise and Fall of Crime in San Francisco

Looking at two decades of SF police data to see what recessions, pandemics, and policy changes actually did to drug offense numbers, and why those numbers can be misleading.

02806 Social Data Analysis & Visualization · Assignment 2 Data Story

San Francisco, like many other U.S. cities, saw decreases in several major crime categories in 2025, including homicides, aggravated assaults, and robberies. According to Mission Local [2], overall crime declined, but drug offences did not follow this trend. San Francisco's drug offence rate increase was the highest of the 19 cities studied in their analysis.

Overdose death rates in San Francisco are higher than both California and the U.S. on average, highlighting a serious drug related public‑health crisis. Data from the San Francisco Chronicle [3] shows that from 2018 to mid‑2025, San Francisco’s monthly overdose death rate was consistently higher through all years. This highlights how drug use and drug markets in San Francisco differ notably from other states and helps explain why drug‑related incidents remain a major concern.

The police department incident reports [1] from 2003 to 2025 can be used to investigate how drug offences have changed over time and whether major events such as the 2008 financial crisis, the 2016 legalisation of recreational marijuana, or the COVID‑19 pandemic may have influenced these patterns.

Next, an examination of spacial patterns of drug offences across San Francisco district. By comparing regions, we aim to identify trends, hotspots, and differences between districts, and clarify how the data might easily appear misleading.

Lastly, utilizing the rich category information in the dataset, the drug offences can be broken down by substance type to gain a clearer picture of how the drugs involved in reported incidents has evolved over time. This helps the understanding of whether the increase in drug offences is driven by mentioned external factors, or rather specific substance trends.

Drug Offenses Over Time

The figure below shows drug offenses reported to SFPD (San Francisco Police Department) per year, broken down by police district. It highlights key historical events to visualize their impact on the rate of reports.

As can be seen on Figure 1, reported drug offenses peaked between 2007 and 2009, closely aligning with the 2008 financial crisis. Following this peak, the data exhibits a steep decline driven by a combination of legislative changes and local challenges. In 2010, the passage of SB 1449 downgraded the possession of under 28.5 grams of marijuana from a misdemeanor to a mere infraction. At the same time, a tampering scandal [7] forced the closure of the San Francisco police drug lab, compelling officers to rely on inexpensive field tests and deterring the costly use of out-of-county testing facilities. This drop in reporting was further compounded by significant staffing reductions and the implementation of California's Public Safety Realignment Act (AB 109), which prompted the city to shift enforcement priorities away from drug offenses and toward violent crime.

However, the most dramatic structural shift occurred immediately after 2016 with the passage of Proposition 64, which legalized recreational marijuana in California, visable as a further drop in recorded incidents. The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent lockdowns in 2020 caused another noticeable dip before the sharp resurgence observed in recent years. This sudden rise in reported offenses is driven by a massive shift in enforcement strategy rather than an overnight surge in crime. Driven to a breaking point by open-air drug markets, SF leaders actively sought outside assistance. This led to the launch of the Drug Market Agency Coordination Center (DMACC) in 2023 and a federal "all hands on deck" crackdown. The chart's steep upward trajectory after 2023 visually captures this renewed enforcement effort.

When breaking down the offenses by police district, the stacked chart reveals that the Tenderloin and Mission districts consistently account for the vast majority of these reports. Interestingly, despite the significant fluctuations in overall incident volume over these two decades, the relative proportion of offenses across districts remains remarkably stable, suggesting that drug enforcement and activity hotspots are deeply ingrained within the city's geography.

Where Crime Concentrates

As visualized by the stacked chart, crime is not spread evenly across the city, as some districts have three to four times as many reports as others. The map below (Figure 2) allows for a deeper understanding of this spatial drug offense distribution. The left map shows each district's drug offense ratio relative to the city-wide baseline. Click any district to update the district-level heatmap on the right, where detailed hotspot information is displayed.

Figure 2 confirms that drug offences are distributed very unevenly across San Francisco, but it also shows why district-level summaries must be interpreted with caution. The district "Mission" sits close to the citywide baseline and therefore serves as a useful visual reference point for the rest of the city. Only Southern and Tenderloin show clearly higher drug offence ratios, while all remaining districts fall below this level. Tenderloin stands out as the most extreme case, with by far the highest relative concentration of reported drug offences, whereas Richmond has the lowest ratio in comparison.

At a first glance, the left (ratio) map seems very informative for determining which areas of San Fransisco has the highest drug related risks, but this district comparison could easily result in overly broad conclusions. For example, Southern appears to be one of the city's most affected districts and could therefore be interpreted as broadly unsafe. However, the district-level heatmap reveals a much more localized pattern. Incidents are concentrated almost entirely on the mainland portion of Southern, while the small island within the district records almost no offences. In this case, the district average hides a strong internal separation that becomes obvious only when the point density (heatmap) is examined as well.

The district "Mission" provides a second example of the same problem. Although its overall ratio lies near the city baseline, the heatmap shows that drug offences are far from evenly spread across the district. More specifically, the Castro Hill side of the district is only weakly represented in the incident data. This means that even a district that appears “average” at the aggregated level can contain both highly concentrated hotspots for drug offences as well as more quiet areas.

Together, the two maps highlight the risk of treating police districts as though they are uniform within their boundaries. District-level ratios can be misleading when used to assess whether an area is safe to visit, walk through, or live in. This also matters from a policy perspective, since targeted policing or intervention strategies based only on district averages may overlook where the risk is actually concentrated and instead direct attention too broadly or to the wrong locations. The heatmap provides that missing local detail and makes clear that drug offences are distributed far more unevenly than the district ratios alone suggest.

These spatial patterns show that the apparent geography of drug crime depends heavily on the level of detail, motivating a closer look at not just where drug offences appear, but more specifically what kinds of drug offences are driving these trends over time.

What's Changed: The Shifting Drug Mix

Understanding how crime changes over time is not just about looking at total numbers; it's about more detailed accounts of what kinds of crimes are being reported. San Francisco's drug crime mix has shifted a lot over the past two decades, and these changes reveal how policy decisions, drug markets, and policing priorities can shape what ends up in the reported data. Figure 3 below visualizes how 9 seperate drug categories have developed over time from 2003 to 2025.

To build this animated graph, the drug specific data was extracted directly from the SF Police Department Incident dataset. Incidents categorised as a "Drug Offense" feature an additional descriptive column detailing the specific reason for the resulting arrest or citation, such as "marijuana”, "methamphetamine”, or "heroin". However, because these descriptions have been recorded by different police officers over many years, the text naturally exhibits a wide array of synonyms, shorthanded abbreviations and spelling errors. To construct a reliable and consistent historical record, we have implemented a mapping process using a custom classification script (checkMapping.py). By manually reviewing the raw data entries, each description was sorted one by one and categorised into standardised drug groups. This ensured that every single reported drug incident was accurately mapped and accounted for, taking all errors into account and providing a clear view of how the city's drug usage has shifted over time.

The plot shows a substantial shift in the composition of San Francisco drug offenses from 2003 to 2024, with the balance moving away from cocaine/crack as the dominator and toward paraphernalia as the main registred drug.

The clearest long-run decline is cocaine/crack which starts at roughly 35-40% of all drug offenses, then trends downward to a mere 6.8% by 2025. This suggests a major structural change in the offense profile over two decades: cocaine/crack, once the largest component, is no longer central by the end of the series. As methamphetamine becomes more central in national enforcement and overdose data, a falling share for cocaine/crack does not necessarily mean cocaine vanished. It means that other substances, especially methamphetamine, accounted for a larger fraction of all recorded incidents.

The most drastic shift involves paraphernalia. After hovering at 15-25% for years, it skyrocketed to nearly half of all offenses by 2025. This large increase reflects a sharp change in enforcement and not necessarily a surge in usage. Historically the municipality's harm reduction focus meant paraphernalia possession was largely "accepted" and rarely penalized. Recently, however, the mayor scaled back these health programs [11] and launched aggressive enforcement campaigns, including the 2025 "Breaking the Cycle" order [12] and a crackdown on illicit corner stores [13]. This targeted police focus suddenly turned a previously ignored offense into the city's primary drug charge.

As the different drug types rise and fall over time, these shifts often reflect bigger changes in policy, availability, and public-health trends. While marijuana-related offences declined after legalization in 2016, other substances like fentanyl, continue to cause major problems in San Francisco. Initiatives such as the AB 109 realignment and Proposition 47 reduced the incentive for officers to write up drug incidents in the same way, even if actual drug activity stayed roughly the same, resulting in the numbers being possibly misleading.

Conclusion

Two decades of San Francisco's drug records reveal a clear reality, incident data measures police enforcement as much as it does criminal behavior. The recent spike in offenses, driven not by a new epidemic but by a sudden crackdown on paraphernalia, demonstrates how swift policy changes can reshape crime statistics. Geography tells a similar story, showing that sweeping district averages often mask hyper localized realities. Ultimately understanding the city's true crime situation requires looking past the raw numbers to see the shifting political and policing priorities that write them.

A note on interpretation: Crime data only captures what gets reported and recorded, not what actually happens. Richardson et al. (2019) make this point well in their work on predictive policing: changes in where police patrol, what they focus on, and how they classify incidents can produce the same statistical signal as a real change in crime. The drug offense decline is a textbook example. For any crime statistic, it is always worth asking: did the crime change, or did the way it was recorded change?
